40. Defines an automated decision tool as any tool that uses artificial intelligence to make consequential decisions relating to employment, education, housing, utilities, healthcare and family planning, financial services, criminal justice, legal services, voting, or government benefits.

Bans the use of automated decision tools in ways that result in algorithmic discrimination on the basis of classes protected by state law. 

Requires any entity that develops or deploys an automated decision tool to perform an impact assessment on the tool, covering its purpose, outputs, use cases, inputted data, and potential discriminatory impacts, and the monitoring, safeguard measures, and testing conducted to mitigate adverse consequences. The impact assessment must be updated annually and after any significant update to the tool, and be provided to the state's Civil Rights Department. 

Requires any entity that deploys an automated decision tool to provide individuals affected by the tool's decisions with a notice that the tool was used and information on the tool's purpose. 

Grants all individuals subject to an automated decision tool's decisions the right to instead have those decisions be made by a human, if technically feasible. 

Requires automated decision tool developers to provide the tool's deployers with documentation of the tool's limitations, training data, and testing. 

Requires the developer or deployer of an automated decision tool to implement a program that governs the foreseeable risks of the tool's use, including designating an employee to monitor the tool and report concerns. 

Permits individuals and public attorneys to bring civil action against entities that violate this law, and makes violation of the law punishable by fines.

Exempts entities with fewer than 25 employees using automated decision tools that affect less than 1,000 people per year from some of this law's provisions.

Encourages partnerships with federal and private entities to enhance AI research and development.

Emphasizes ethical considerations, cybersecurity, and data privacy in AI projects.

Allocates $300 million annually from 2025 to 2030 for these initiatives.

Requires an assessment of energy security related to data centers and computing resources, with recommendations for policy changes.

49. Enacts the "Freedom From AI-Rigged (FAIR) Elections Act" to regulate synthetic media in electioneering communications.

Defines "synthetic media" as altered audio or video intended to mislead about real events or speech through advanced digital techniques.

Allows candidates misrepresented by synthetic media to seek injunctive relief and damages against the creators or distributors.

Requires disclosures on synthetic media: a visible text or audible notice stating the media is manipulated.

Places the burden on plaintiffs to prove synthetic media use by clear and convincing evidence.

Holds information content providers liable, not the medium, unless disclosures are altered or removed.

Encourages courts to resolve cases swiftly.

Declares provisions severable to maintain the act's enforceability if parts are invalidated.

Implements the act immediately upon passage due to an emergency declaration.

50. Establishes a single state data governance structure, creating a federated data platform to manage data effectively across Rhode Island state agencies.

Mandates the Department of Administration (DOA) to appoint a Chief Data Officer (CDO) to oversee the Data Center of Excellence, enhancing data operations and readiness for AI integration.

Creates an AI Center of Excellence to formulate AI policies and ethics, minimizing bias and guiding AI implementation in state operations.

Directs all state agencies to participate in the federated data system, sharing data for policy analysis and exploring AI applications to improve operations.

Forms an AI Task Force to assess AI risks and opportunities, providing advice to policymakers.

Instructs the Department of Labor and Training (DLT), Rhode Island Department of Education (RIDE), and Office of Post Secondary Commissioner (OPC) to develop AI training programs for state and private-sector workers, integrating AI topics into existing curricula.
